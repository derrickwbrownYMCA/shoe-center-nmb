Shoe Center NMB Rebrand Vision Document and Technical Specification — Version 2.1, March 2026
Executive Summary
Shoe Center NMB will evolve from a legacy, service-led local shoe destination into a modern, lifestyle-oriented e-commerce boutique that still feels unmistakably rooted in the North Myrtle Beach community. The brand foundation is already strong: a family-owned-and-operated store since 1978 (48 years as of 2026), with deep credibility in comfort, fashion, and dance footwear, and a documented history of high-touch customer service (including national recognition from Footwear Insight’s Gold Medal Service Awards). 

This new Shopify experience will lean into “We Make Your Feet Happy” as both promise and system: a fast, clean, mobile-first shopping journey that reduces uncertainty (fit, comfort, occasion) and increases confidence (social proof, guidance, omnichannel pickup). It must expand men’s visibility and sales mix while staying loyal to Shag dancers—especially important in this region where the Shag is formally recognized in South Carolina as the official state dance (Act 329 of 1984) and culturally associated with the Ocean Drive area in North Myrtle Beach.

Brand Identity and Visual Direction
Tagline and brand story

Tagline: We Make Your Feet Happy. This line already appears in public-facing promotional content and can function as a measurable brand promise when supported with fit guidance, comfort education, and low-friction fulfillment. 

Brand story (recommended narrative arc):
Shoe Center NMB is a family-owned comfort, fashion, and dance shoe destination “since 1978,” built on old-school service and expertise. 
 The new brand expression modernizes the look without losing the heart: a coastal-Southern boutique that helps customers feel confident in what they buy—whether they’re headed to brunch, the boardwalk, work, or a night of Shag. Service isn’t a slogan; it’s proven and recognized, including Shoe Center’s 2021 Gold Medal Service Award recognition by Footwear Insight, which was based on mystery-shop criteria throughout the buying process (greeting, measuring, checkout quality, and more).

Typography system (Shopify-ready, performance-aware)
Goal: premium but approachable, with high legibility on mobile and a soft coastal vibe.

Recommended stack (Google-font compatible + self-hostable):

Headings: Fraunces (600–700) — warm serif with boutique personality.
Body/UI: Inter (400–600) — modern, highly readable, neutral.
Accent microtype (optional): Inter (600, uppercase, letter-spaced) — avoids a third font while still creating hierarchy.

Mood board directives (developer/creative-ready):

Lighting: golden hour + bright overcast; avoid harsh flash; keep skin tones warm and natural.
Locations: boardwalks, beach paths, marinas, pastel storefronts, porches, airy interiors with plants and textured neutrals. Include subtle local cues (signage, dunes, beach grass) without becoming “tourist merch.”
People (30–50 priority): show couples, friends, and families; include men prominently and confidently; keep posing candid (walking, laughing, stepping out of a car, heading to dinner).
Shag representation (tasteful): one or two stills that feel documentary—shoes on a dance floor, a couple from behind, or a close-up of footwork—kept secondary to broader lifestyle.
Product framing: always show shoes in context (walking, standing, dancing) plus crisp product-only images. For comfort credibility, include “downward angle” shots that show footbed, arch, straps, and outsole grip.
Color mood: sand, white, faded denim blues, sea-glass greens, occasional coral pop. This is driven by the references’ coastal palette and “resort” storytelling cues.

Overall design principles (Do’s and Don’ts)
Do:

Keep the interface calm and airy; let photography and whitespace carry the lifestyle tone.
Use a comfort-first shopping frame: fit info near purchase actions, reviews visible early, and clear pickup/shipping promises.
Bias toward men’s visibility through layout (not aggressive “Men!” messaging): equal hero CTA weight, men’s featured rail, and men’s editorial imagery.
Don’t:

Don’t use flashy effects, auto-rotating hero carousels, or video-heavy content (performance + tone risk).
Don’t bury fit/sizing in an FAQ—surface it at the exact point of decision.
Don’t make Shag the homepage headline; it should feel like an “insider layer,” not the main pitch.
Target Audience and Personas
These personas are designed to influence information architecture, photography emphasis, merchandising rules, and UX prioritization.

Persona A: “Coastal Professional, Comfort-First” (Female, 36–45, local/regional)
Goals: find stylish shoes that can handle long days; trust the store’s fit expertise; buy quickly on mobile; pick up locally when possible.
Pain points: uncertainty about arch support/comfort; worries about returns; wants style but refuses “hurts to wear it.”
Design implications: persistent comfort cues (arch support, cushioning level, width notes); “Fit Help” always within one tap; pickup availability visible on PDPs; clean, non-pushy promotions.

Persona B: “Vacation Dad Who Actually Shops” (Male, 38–50, visitor + occasional local repeat)
Goals: buy breathable, good-looking shoes/sandals he can walk in all day; shop fast; avoid guessing sizing; wants men’s products to feel “for him,” not like an afterthought.
Pain points: men’s categories hidden on many boutiques; limited time on vacation; skeptically avoids “boutique sites” that feel female-only.
Design implications: top-nav Men; homepage hero with dual CTA (Shop Men / Shop Women); men’s best sellers above the fold; simple filters (size, width, slip-on, waterproof, travel); fit recommendations built into PDP.

Persona C: “Shag Loyalist Couple” (Ages 55–70, local/core customer base)
Goals: find dance-appropriate shoes and buy from trusted experts; feel respected (not rebranded out of existence); stay connected to community traditions and seasonal events.
Pain points: modern e-commerce can feel confusing or impersonal; worries dance categories disappear; needs fast answers when an event is approaching.
Design implications: dedicated Dance + “Shag Community” area; “call the store” and “fit help” surfaced as assistive options; tasteful event + community cues; maintain familiar terminology (Shag / Ballroom / Swing / Line). 

Business Goals and Success Metrics
Below are practical, trackable KPIs paired with the specific site features designed to move them.

Grow men’s sales to ~50% of revenue (in-store + online)
Success metrics: men’s revenue share, men’s conversion rate, men’s AOV, repeat purchase rate for men.
Feature drivers: Men-first navigation + homepage CTA parity; men’s “Best Sellers” rail; men’s fit filters (width, arch support, slip-on); men’s lifestyle photography at hero + collection top.

Increase overall conversion rate with lower fit uncertainty
Success metrics: conversion rate by device; add-to-cart rate; product page exit rate; return rate (especially “didn’t fit/comfort”).
Feature drivers: structured size/fit system and clear sizing information (Baymard’s apparel benchmark indicates most apparel sites fail to provide sufficient sizing info—an avoidable conversion leak); size selection UI patterns (buttons vs. dropdowns); fit notes and “true to size” review aggregation. 

Protect and grow dance/Shag loyalty while keeping it secondary
Success metrics: Dance collection conversion rate; returning customer rate in Dance segment; email segment engagement; event-period sales lifts.
Feature drivers: Dance landing page; Shag Community page; subtle “Shag-Ready” product badges; seasonal merchandising aligned to known SOS dates (without turning the brand into an event site). 

Increase omnichannel convenience (BOPIS / pickup)
Success metrics: share of orders using store pickup; pickup availability interactions on PDP; in-store pickup fulfillment times; customer satisfaction.
Feature drivers: Shopify pickup in store configuration + PDP pickup availability UI; clear microcopy at checkout and PDP; POS workflows for pickup orders. 

Maintain high-speed, premium UX standards
Success metrics: Lighthouse ≥95 (mobile + desktop); Core Web Vitals pass rate; LCP/INP/CLS at 75th percentile; bounce rate from mobile product pages.
Feature drivers: minimal JS, performance budgets, controlled third-party apps; image optimization; accessibility-first UI (which also reduces UX friction). Core Web Vitals thresholds: LCP <2.5s, INP <200ms, CLS <0.1; INP replaced FID as the responsiveness metric as of March 2024.

Fit guide and size recommendation system
Fit uncertainty is one of the highest-leverage conversion problems in apparel/footwear UX; Baymard reports widespread failure across sites to provide sufficient sizing information, which increases abandonment risk. 

Specification (minimum viable, high-impact):

Size selections must be button-based, not only dropdowns (mobile-friendly and clearer). 
Size Guide link placed adjacent to size selector with measurement instructions (inches + cm) and width guidance.
PDP Fit Summary (metafields-driven):
True to size? (Yes/No/Size up/Size down)
Width notes (narrow/regular/wide)
Arch support level (1–5)
Cushioning level (1–5)
Foot concerns supported (bunion-friendly, plantar support, etc.)
Recommendation system (phaseable):

Phase 1 (Theme-only): rules-based recommendations derived from metafields (e.g., “Runs small → consider half size up”).
Phase 2 (Enhanced): lightweight Fit Finder quiz to suggest size + product category based on user inputs; optionally layer in review-derived “fit” aggregation if supported by the reviews provider.

Technical, Performance, SEO, Accessibility, and Content Standards
Platform and theme architecture (Shopify Online Store 2.0)
Platform: Shopify Online Store 2.0, Liquid theme (not headless). OS 2.0 enables JSON templates (“sections everywhere”) so sections can be added/removed across most pages via Theme Editor, improving maintainability. 

Theme build rules:

All custom sections must include schema presets to be addable in the editor. 
Use Theme Editor blocks heavily (buttons, image blocks, icon + text) so marketing can iterate without code edits. 
Use dynamic sources/metafields so product fit content and badges are editable without developer involvement. 
Mobile-first responsive specifications
Target “primary device” assumptions:

Mobile widths: 360–430px common; design for thumb reach and one-hand use.
Tap targets: minimum 44x44px; space controls to avoid accidental taps (aligns with modern accessibility expectations). 
Layout rules:

Product cards: 2-up grid on mobile collections; ensure price, rating, and quick “Men/Women/Dance” cues remain visible.
Sticky PDP purchase bar: show price, selected variant, and Add to Cart; never cover critical content or focus states.
Use responsive images with srcset and correct sizing; preload hero image; lazy-load below fold.
Accessibility standard (WCAG 2.2 AA)
Accessibility must meet WCAG 2.2 Level AA requirements. 

Implementation checklist (practical):

Visible focus states that are not obscured by sticky headers/bars and meet new WCAG 2.2 expectations. 
Color contrast: all body text and actionable UI meets minimum contrast; do not rely on color alone for meaning. 
Keyboard navigability for menus, modals (size guide), accordion content, and cart drawer.
Accessible form labels and inline error handling for email/SMS opt-ins and contact forms.
SEO best practices (Shopify + Google) Key structure rules:

Keep navigation and internal linking shallow where possible; Shopify explicitly recommends that pages not be more than 3 clicks away from the home page when feasible. 
Use descriptive menu labels and internal link text (not vague “Click here”). 
Blogging supports SEO and community-building using Shopify’s built-in blog engine. 
Structured data:

Add/maintain Product structured data so search results can display richer product details (price, availability, ratings). 
Shopify provides a structured_data Liquid filter for product and article objects; use it carefully and avoid duplicate schemas from apps. 
Merchant feeds (footwear-specific practicality):

For shoes, Google’s product data requirements can require proper brand plus GTIN or MPN values; missing identifiers can cause issues. 
Theme Editor customization guidelines (merchant autonomy without performance loss)
Guardrails:

Limit the number of “heavy” sections allowed on homepage (e.g., carousels, external embeds).
Provide “Simple / Standard / Editorial” presets for key templates (Home, PDP, About) so the team can switch layouts safely.
Create a “Performance-safe” section library (static images + text + product rails) and a “Use sparingly” bucket (social feeds, multi-row sliders).
Content and tone guidelines (voice + samples)

Be concrete: comfort features, fit notes, pickup timing, and returns clarity.
